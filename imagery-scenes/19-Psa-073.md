# Psalm 73
1: מִזְמ֗וֹר לְאָ֫סָ֥ף אַ֤ךְ ט֭וֹב לְיִשְׂרָאֵ֥ל אֱלֹהִ֗ים לְבָרֵ֥י לֵבָֽב׃
2: וַאֲנִ֗י כִּ֭מְעַט רַגְלָ֑י כְּ֝אַ֗יִן אֲשֻׁרָֽי׃
3: כִּֽי־ קִ֭נֵּאתִי בַּֽהוֹלְלִ֑ים שְׁל֖וֹם רְשָׁעִ֣ים אֶרְאֶֽה׃
4: כִּ֤י אֵ֖ין חַרְצֻבּ֥וֹת לְמוֹתָ֗ם וּבָרִ֥יא אוּלָֽם׃
5: בַּעֲמַ֣ל אֱנ֣וֹשׁ אֵינֵ֑מוֹ וְעִם־ אָ֝דָ֗ם לֹ֣א יְנֻגָּֽעוּ׃
6: לָ֭כֵן עֲנָקַ֣תְמוֹ גַאֲוָ֑ה יַעֲטָף־ שִׁ֝֗ית חָמָ֥ס לָֽמוֹ׃
7: יָ֭צָא מֵחֵ֣לֶב עֵינֵ֑מוֹ עָ֝בְר֗וּ מַשְׂכִּיּ֥וֹת לֵבָֽב׃
8: יָמִ֤יקוּ ׀ וִידַבְּר֣וּ בְרָ֣ע עֹ֑שֶׁק מִמָּר֥וֹם יְדַבֵּֽרוּ׃
9: שַׁתּ֣וּ בַשָּׁמַ֣יִם פִּיהֶ֑ם וּ֝לְשׁוֹנָ֗ם תִּֽהֲלַ֥ךְ בָּאָֽרֶץ׃
10: לָכֵ֤ן ׀ עַמּ֣וֹ הֲלֹ֑ם וּמֵ֥י מָ֝לֵ֗א יִמָּ֥צוּ לָֽמוֹ׃
11: וְֽאָמְר֗וּ אֵיכָ֥ה יָדַֽע־ אֵ֑ל וְיֵ֖שׁ דֵּעָ֣ה בְעֶלְיֽוֹן׃
12: הִנֵּה־ אֵ֥לֶּה רְשָׁעִ֑ים וְשַׁלְוֵ֥י ע֝וֹלָ֗ם הִשְׂגּוּ־ חָֽיִל׃
13: אַךְ־ רִ֭יק זִכִּ֣יתִי לְבָבִ֑י וָאֶרְחַ֖ץ בְּנִקָּי֣וֹן כַּפָּֽי׃
14: וָאֱהִ֣י נָ֭גוּעַ כָּל־ הַיּ֑וֹם וְ֝תוֹכַחְתִּ֗י לַבְּקָרִֽים׃
15: אִם־ אָ֭מַרְתִּי אֲסַפְּרָ֥ה כְמ֑וֹ הִנֵּ֤ה ד֭וֹר בָּנֶ֣יךָ בָגָֽדְתִּי׃
16: וָֽ֭אֲחַשְּׁבָה לָדַ֣עַת זֹ֑את עָמָ֖ל בְעֵינָֽי׃
17: עַד־ אָ֭בוֹא אֶל־ מִקְדְּשֵׁי־ אֵ֑ל אָ֝בִ֗ינָה לְאַחֲרִיתָֽם׃
18: אַ֣ךְ בַּ֭חֲלָקוֹת תָּשִׁ֣ית לָ֑מוֹ הִ֝פַּלְתָּ֗ם לְמַשּׁוּאֽוֹת׃
19: אֵ֤יךְ הָי֣וּ לְשַׁמָּ֣ה כְרָ֑גַע סָ֥פוּ תַ֝֗מּוּ מִן־ בַּלָּהֽוֹת׃
20: כַּחֲל֥וֹם מֵהָקִ֑יץ אֲ֝דֹנָי בָּעִ֤יר ׀ צַלְמָ֬ם תִּבְזֶֽה׃
21: כִּ֭י יִתְחַמֵּ֣ץ לְבָבִ֑י וְ֝כִלְיוֹתַ֗י אֶשְׁתּוֹנָֽן׃
22: וַאֲנִי־ בַ֭עַר וְלֹ֣א אֵדָ֑ע בְּ֝הֵמ֗וֹת הָיִ֥יתִי עִמָּֽךְ׃
23: וַאֲנִ֣י תָמִ֣יד עִמָּ֑ךְ אָ֝חַ֗זְתָּ בְּיַד־ יְמִינִֽי׃
24: בַּעֲצָתְךָ֥ תַנְחֵ֑נִי וְ֝אַחַ֗ר כָּב֥וֹד תִּקָּחֵֽנִי׃
25: מִי־ לִ֥י בַשָּׁמָ֑יִם וְ֝עִמְּךָ֗ לֹא־ חָפַ֥צְתִּי בָאָֽרֶץ׃
26: כָּלָ֥ה שְׁאֵרִ֗י וּלְבָ֫בִ֥י צוּר־ לְבָבִ֥י וְחֶלְקִ֗י אֱלֹהִ֥ים לְעוֹלָֽם׃
27: כִּֽי־ הִנֵּ֣ה רְחֵקֶ֣יךָ יֹאבֵ֑דוּ הִ֝צְמַ֗תָּה כָּל־ זוֹנֶ֥ה מִמֶּֽךָּ׃
28: וַאֲנִ֤י ׀ קִֽרֲבַ֥ת אֱלֹהִ֗ים לִ֫י־ ט֥וֹב שַׁתִּ֤י ׀ בַּאדֹנָ֣י יְהֹוִ֣ה מַחְסִ֑י לְ֝סַפֵּ֗ר כָּל־ מַלְאֲכוֹתֶֽיךָ׃

# Imagery Outline of Psalm 73

## Scene 1: Envy of the Prosperous

> Surely God is good to Israel, to those who are pure in heart. But as for me, my feet had almost slipped; I had nearly lost my foothold. For I envied the arrogant when I saw the prosperity of the wicked. - Psalm 73:1-3 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist, who is described as pure in heart, expresses that his faith has been shaken.
  - He admits to feeling envious of the wicked and their prosperity, to the point where he almost lost his faith.

- Imagery:
  - The image of slipping and losing one's foothold signifies the psalmist's struggle with his faith in the face of the apparent success of the wicked.

## Scene 2: Prosperity of the Wicked

> They have no struggles; their bodies are healthy and strong. They are free from common human burdens; they are not plagued by human ills. - Psalm 73:4-5 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist describes the seemingly carefree and prosperous lives of the wicked.
  - He highlights their good health, lack of common burdens, and freedom from troubles.

- Imagery:
  - The imagery of healthy and strong bodies suggests that the wicked enjoy physical well-being.
  - The absence of common human burdens and ills implies a life of ease and comfort for the wicked.

## Scene 3: Pride and Arrogance

> Therefore pride is their necklace; they clothe themselves with violence. From their callous hearts comes iniquity; their evil imaginations have no limits. They scoff, and speak with malice; with arrogance they threaten oppression. - Psalm 73:6-8 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist describes the wicked as being characterized by pride, violence, and arrogance.
  - He emphasizes their callous hearts and limitless evil imaginations.
  - He mentions their scoffing, speaking with malice, and threatening oppression.

- Imagery:
  - The imagery of wearing pride as a necklace symbolizes the wicked's arrogance and self-importance.
  - The clothes of violence suggest that the wicked use their power and strength to harm others.
  - The image of callous hearts portrays the lack of empathy and compassion in the wicked.
  - The reference to evil imaginations with no limits implies the extent of the wicked's wickedness.
  - The use of scoffing, speaking with malice, and threatening oppression signifies the wicked's disregard for others and their intention to exert power over them.

## Scene 4: Doubts and Temptation

> Their mouths lay claim to heaven, and their tongues take possession of the earth. Therefore their people turn to them and drink up waters in abundance. They say, "How would God know? Does the Most High know anything?" This is what the wicked are like—always free of care, they go on amassing wealth. - Psalm 73:9-12 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist expresses the doubts and temptations that arise from observing the prosperity of the wicked.
  - He mentions their arrogant speech, their influence over others, and their disbelief in God's knowledge.
  - He acknowledges their carefree attitude and the continuous accumulation of wealth.

- Imagery:
  - The image of the wicked claiming heaven and possessing the earth with their mouths and tongues denotes their arrogance and boastful speech.
  - The reference to their followers drinking up waters in abundance suggests that the wicked provide abundance and prosperity to those who follow them.
  - The questioning of God's knowledge implies that the wicked doubt God's awareness of their actions.
  - The idea of the wicked always being free of care highlights their carefree and unburdened lifestyles.
  - The accumulation of wealth signifies the wicked's relentless pursuit and enjoyment of material possessions.

## Scene 5: An Inner Struggle

> Surely in vain I have kept my heart pure and have washed my hands in innocence. All day long I have been afflicted, and every morning brings new punishments. - Psalm 73:13-14 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist expresses his struggle and doubts about the value of his faith and righteous living.
  - He laments being afflicted and facing punishment repeatedly.

- Imagery:
  - The reference to keeping his heart pure and washing his hands in innocence indicates the psalmist's commitment to righteousness.
  - The all-day affliction and new punishments every morning depict the psalmist's ongoing struggles and hardships.

## Scene 6: Questioning God's Justice

> If I had spoken out like that, I would have betrayed your children. When I tried to understand all this, it troubled me deeply. - Psalm 73:15-16 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist reflects on the consequences of expressing doubts and turning away from God.
  - He confesses that trying to understand the situation troubled him greatly.

- Imagery:
  - The image of betraying God's children signifies the negative impact of speaking out against God and succumbing to doubts.
  - The troubling thoughts and deep inner turmoil symbolize the psalmist's internal struggle to comprehend God's justice.

## Scene 7: Seeking Understanding

> Till I entered the sanctuary of God; then I understood their final destiny. Surely you place them on slippery ground; you cast them down to ruin. How suddenly are they destroyed, completely swept away by terrors! - Psalm 73:17-19 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist finds clarity and understanding when he enters the sanctuary of God.
  - He acknowledges God's role in the ultimate destiny of the wicked.
  - He recognizes that the wicked are on slippery ground, destined for ruin and sudden destruction.

- Imagery:
  - The image of entering the sanctuary of God represents the psalmist seeking and finding spiritual enlightenment and understanding.
  - The reference to slippery ground and being cast down to ruin implies the precarious and fleeting nature of the wicked's prosperity.
  - The sudden destruction and being swept away by terrors depicts the swift and inevitable judgment that awaits the wicked.

## Scene 8: Gratitude for God's Presence

> They are like a dream when one awakes; when you arise, Lord, you will despise them as fantasies. When my heart was grieved and my spirit embittered, I was senseless and ignorant; I was a brute beast before you. - Psalm 73:20-22 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist compares the wicked to a dream that fades upon waking.
  - He expresses his gratitude for God's presence and acknowledges his previous ignorance and foolishness.

- Imagery:
  - The image of a dream fading upon awakening reflects the impermanence and unreality of the wicked's prosperity.
  - The reference to being senseless and ignorant, like a brute beast, signifies the psalmist's recognition of his own folly and lack of understanding.
 
## Scene 9: Trusting in God's Guidance

> Yet I am always with you; you hold me by my right hand. You guide me with your counsel, and afterward you will take me into glory. Whom have I in heaven but you? And earth has nothing I desire besides you. My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever. - Psalm 73:23-26 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist affirms his constant presence with God and God's guidance in his life.
  - He declares his trust in God and acknowledges the ultimate glory that awaits him.
  - He emphasizes his desire for God above all else and his reliance on God's strength.

- Imagery:
  - The image of being held by the right hand suggests God's intimate presence and guidance in the psalmist's life.
  - The idea of being taken into glory signifies the psalmist's confidence in the afterlife and eternal reward.
  - The declaration of desiring God above all else portrays the psalmist's deep longing for a relationship with God.
  - The mention of failing flesh and heart showcases the psalmist's recognition of his own frailty and reliance on God's strength.

## Scene 10: God's Judgment on the Wicked

> Those who are far from you will perish; you destroy all who are unfaithful to you. But as for me, it is good to be near God. I have made the Sovereign Lord my refuge; I will tell of all your deeds. - Psalm 73:27-28 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist acknowledges God's judgment on those who are unfaithful and far from Him.
  - He affirms the goodness of being near God and making God his refuge.
  - He commits to proclaiming and praising God for His deeds.

- Imagery:
  - The image of those far from God perishing symbolizes the ultimate fate of the wicked who reject God.
  - The idea of being near God and finding refuge in Him denotes the psalmist's sense of security and closeness to God.
  - The commitment to telling of God's deeds represents the psalmist's desire to share God's greatness and faithfulness with others.

## Summary

The scenes in this Psalm depict the struggle of the psalmist as he observes the prosperity of the wicked. He wrestles with doubts, envy, and the temptation to abandon his faith. But through seeking understanding and entering God's sanctuary, the psalmist gains clarity and finds solace. He realizes the fleeting nature of the wicked's success and places his trust in God's guidance and strength. The psalmist ultimately acknowledges the judgment that awaits the unfaithful and commits to proclaiming God's deeds while finding refuge in Him. The overall message of the psalm is to trust in God's justice and place our hope in Him, despite the challenges and temptations of this world.
