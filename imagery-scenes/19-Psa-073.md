# Psalm 73
1: מִזְמ֗וֹר לְאָ֫סָ֥ף אַ֤ךְ ט֭וֹב לְיִשְׂרָאֵ֥ל אֱלֹהִ֗ים לְבָרֵ֥י לֵבָֽב׃
2: וַאֲנִ֗י כִּ֭מְעַט רַגְלָ֑י כְּ֝אַ֗יִן אֲשֻׁרָֽי׃
3: כִּֽי־ קִ֭נֵּאתִי בַּֽהוֹלְלִ֑ים שְׁל֖וֹם רְשָׁעִ֣ים אֶרְאֶֽה׃
4: כִּ֤י אֵ֖ין חַרְצֻבּ֥וֹת לְמוֹתָ֗ם וּבָרִ֥יא אוּלָֽם׃
5: בַּעֲמַ֣ל אֱנ֣וֹשׁ אֵינֵ֑מוֹ וְעִם־ אָ֝דָ֗ם לֹ֣א יְנֻגָּֽעוּ׃
6: לָ֭כֵן עֲנָקַ֣תְמוֹ גַאֲוָ֑ה יַעֲטָף־ שִׁ֝֗ית חָמָ֥ס לָֽמוֹ׃
7: יָ֭צָא מֵחֵ֣לֶב עֵינֵ֑מוֹ עָ֝בְר֗וּ מַשְׂכִּיּ֥וֹת לֵבָֽב׃
8: יָמִ֤יקוּ ׀ וִידַבְּר֣וּ בְרָ֣ע עֹ֑שֶׁק מִמָּר֥וֹם יְדַבֵּֽרוּ׃
9: שַׁתּ֣וּ בַשָּׁמַ֣יִם פִּיהֶ֑ם וּ֝לְשׁוֹנָ֗ם תִּֽהֲלַ֥ךְ בָּאָֽרֶץ׃
10: לָכֵ֤ן ׀ עַמּ֣וֹ הֲלֹ֑ם וּמֵ֥י מָ֝לֵ֗א יִמָּ֥צוּ לָֽמוֹ׃
11: וְֽאָמְר֗וּ אֵיכָ֥ה יָדַֽע־ אֵ֑ל וְיֵ֖שׁ דֵּעָ֣ה בְעֶלְיֽוֹן׃
12: הִנֵּה־ אֵ֥לֶּה רְשָׁעִ֑ים וְשַׁלְוֵ֥י ע֝וֹלָ֗ם הִשְׂגּוּ־ חָֽיִל׃
13: אַךְ־ רִ֭יק זִכִּ֣יתִי לְבָבִ֑י וָאֶרְחַ֖ץ בְּנִקָּי֣וֹן כַּפָּֽי׃
14: וָאֱהִ֣י נָ֭גוּעַ כָּל־ הַיּ֑וֹם וְ֝תוֹכַחְתִּ֗י לַבְּקָרִֽים׃
15: אִם־ אָ֭מַרְתִּי אֲסַפְּרָ֥ה כְמ֑וֹ הִנֵּ֤ה ד֭וֹר בָּנֶ֣יךָ בָגָֽדְתִּי׃
16: וָֽ֭אֲחַשְּׁבָה לָדַ֣עַת זֹ֑את עָמָ֖ל בְעֵינָֽי׃
17: עַד־ אָ֭בוֹא אֶל־ מִקְדְּשֵׁי־ אֵ֑ל אָ֝בִ֗ינָה לְאַחֲרִיתָֽם׃
18: אַ֣ךְ בַּ֭חֲלָקוֹת תָּשִׁ֣ית לָ֑מוֹ הִ֝פַּלְתָּ֗ם לְמַשּׁוּאֽוֹת׃
19: אֵ֤יךְ הָי֣וּ לְשַׁמָּ֣ה כְרָ֑גַע סָ֥פוּ תַ֝֗מּוּ מִן־ בַּלָּהֽוֹת׃
20: כַּחֲל֥וֹם מֵהָקִ֑יץ אֲ֝דֹנָי בָּעִ֤יר ׀ צַלְמָ֬ם תִּבְזֶֽה׃
21: כִּ֭י יִתְחַמֵּ֣ץ לְבָבִ֑י וְ֝כִלְיוֹתַ֗י אֶשְׁתּוֹנָֽן׃
22: וַאֲנִי־ בַ֭עַר וְלֹ֣א אֵדָ֑ע בְּ֝הֵמ֗וֹת הָיִ֥יתִי עִמָּֽךְ׃
23: וַאֲנִ֣י תָמִ֣יד עִמָּ֑ךְ אָ֝חַ֗זְתָּ בְּיַד־ יְמִינִֽי׃
24: בַּעֲצָתְךָ֥ תַנְחֵ֑נִי וְ֝אַחַ֗ר כָּב֥וֹד תִּקָּחֵֽנִי׃
25: מִי־ לִ֥י בַשָּׁמָ֑יִם וְ֝עִמְּךָ֗ לֹא־ חָפַ֥צְתִּי בָאָֽרֶץ׃
26: כָּלָ֥ה שְׁאֵרִ֗י וּלְבָ֫בִ֥י צוּר־ לְבָבִ֥י וְחֶלְקִ֗י אֱלֹהִ֥ים לְעוֹלָֽם׃
27: כִּֽי־ הִנֵּ֣ה רְחֵקֶ֣יךָ יֹאבֵ֑דוּ הִ֝צְמַ֗תָּה כָּל־ זוֹנֶ֥ה מִמֶּֽךָּ׃
28: וַאֲנִ֤י ׀ קִֽרֲבַ֥ת אֱלֹהִ֗ים לִ֫י־ ט֥וֹב שַׁתִּ֤י ׀ בַּאדֹנָ֣י יְהֹוִ֣ה מַחְסִ֑י לְ֝סַפֵּ֗ר כָּל־ מַלְאֲכוֹתֶֽיךָ׃

# Imagery Outline of Psalm 73

## Scene 1: Envy of the Prosperous

> "Surely God is good to Israel, to those who are pure in heart. But as for me, my feet had almost slipped; I had nearly lost my foothold. For I envied the arrogant when I saw the prosperity of the wicked." - Psalm 73:1-3 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist begins by acknowledging God's goodness to the pure in heart.
  - The psalmist confesses that they were tempted to slip and lose their footing due to envy.
- Imagery:
  - God's goodness to Israel and the pure in heart is depicted as a source of stability and security.
  - The psalmist's slipping and nearly losing their foothold symbolizes their struggle with the temptation of envy.
  - The prosperity of the wicked serves as the object of envy, highlighting the contrast between their worldly success and the psalmist's own struggles.

## Scene 2: The Prosperity of the Wicked

> "They have no struggles; their bodies are healthy and strong. They are free from common human burdens; they are not plagued by human ills. Therefore pride is their necklace; they clothe themselves with violence. From their callous hearts comes iniquity; their evil imaginations have no limits." - Psalm 73:4-7 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist describes the prosperous wicked who seem to have no struggles and are in good health.
  - The wicked are portrayed as free from burdens and afflictions that affect ordinary humans.
  - The wicked are depicted as being prideful, violent, and filled with limitless evil imaginations.
- Imagery:
  - The health and strength of the wicked emphasize their material well-being and apparent invulnerability.
  - The absence of common human burdens and afflictions symbolizes the carefree life that the wicked enjoy.
  - The wicked wearing pride as a necklace and clothing themselves with violence symbolize their arrogance and propensity for cruelty.
  - The callous hearts and limitless evil imaginations of the wicked illustrate the depths of their corruption and moral decay.

## Scene 3: Temptation to Despair

> "They scoff, and speak with malice; with arrogance they threaten oppression. Their mouths lay claim to heaven, and their tongues take possession of the earth. Therefore their people turn to them and drink up waters in abundance. They say, 'How would God know? Does the Most High know anything?'" - Psalm 73:8-11 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist observes the wicked scoffing, speaking with malice, and threatening oppression.
  - The wicked are depicted as arrogantly claiming authority over heaven and earth.
  - The followers of the wicked are described as turning to them and seeking their abundance.
  - The wicked question God's knowledge and authority.
- Imagery:
  - The scoffing and malice of the wicked reflect their disdain for righteousness and their desire to harm others.
  - The arrogant claim to control heaven and earth represents the wicked's deluded sense of power and entitlement.
  - The followers drinking up waters in abundance symbolizes their reliance on the wicked for their material needs and desires.
  - The questioning of God's knowledge and authority reveals the wicked's disregard for divine judgment and accountability.

## Scene 4: The Psalmist's Struggle and Doubt

> "This is what the wicked are like—always free of care, they go on amassing wealth. Surely in vain I have kept my heart pure and have washed my hands in innocence. All day long I have been afflicted, and every morning brings new punishments." - Psalm 73:12-14 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist reiterates the carefree nature of the wicked's life and their continuous accumulation of wealth.
  - The psalmist expresses doubt and frustration, questioning the value of their own righteous living.
  - The psalmist describes their personal affliction and suffering.
- Imagery:
  - The continuous amassing of wealth by the wicked emphasizes the unfairness and apparent lack of consequences for their actions.
  - The psalmist's questioning of the value of their purity of heart and innocence highlights their internal struggle with doubt and disillusionment.
  - The psalmist's affliction and daily punishments symbolize the hardships they endure while witnessing the prosperity of the wicked.

## Scene 5: Understanding in the Sanctuary

> "If I had spoken out like that, I would have betrayed your children. When I tried to understand all this, it troubled me deeply till I entered the sanctuary of God; then I understood their final destiny." - Psalm 73:15-17 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist reflects on the consequences of speaking out against the wicked and betraying God's children.
  - The psalmist confesses their deep trouble in trying to understand the situation.
  - The psalmist finds understanding and resolution in the sanctuary of God.
- Imagery:
  - Speaking out against the wicked and betraying God's children symbolizes a deviation from righteousness and loyalty to God.
  - The deep trouble experienced by the psalmist represents the internal turmoil and confusion caused by their inability to comprehend the prosperity of the wicked.
  - Entering the sanctuary of God symbolizes seeking spiritual refuge and finding wisdom and understanding in the presence of God.
  - Understanding the final destiny of the wicked signifies gaining insight into the ultimate justice and judgment that awaits them.

## Scene 6: The Fragility of the Wicked's Prosperity

> "Surely you place them on slippery ground; you cast them down to ruin. How suddenly are they destroyed, completely swept away by terrors! They are like a dream when one awakes; when you arise, Lord, you will despise them as fantasies." - Psalm 73:18-20 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist acknowledges that God has the power to bring the wicked to ruin despite their prosperity.
  - The sudden and complete destruction of the wicked is described, emphasizing the fragility of their success.
  - The temporary nature of the wicked's existence is compared to a dream or fantasy.
- Imagery:
  - God placing the wicked on slippery ground and casting them down to ruin symbolize their vulnerability and eventual downfall.
  - The sudden destruction and terrifying fate of the wicked illustrate the impermanence and fleeting nature of their worldly prosperity.
  - Comparing the wicked's existence to a dream or fantasy suggests that their success and power hold no lasting significance or substance.

## Scene 7: Trust and God's Guidance

> "When my heart was grieved and my spirit embittered, I was senseless and ignorant; I was a brute beast before you. Yet I am always with you; you hold me by my right hand. You guide me with your counsel, and afterward you will take me into glory. Whom have I in heaven but you? And earth has nothing I desire besides you." - Psalm 73:21-25 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist acknowledges their previous state of grief, bitterness, and spiritual ignorance.
  - The psalmist recognizes their constant presence with God and God's guidance in their life.
  - The psalmist expresses trust in God's counsel and the assurance of future glory.
  - The psalmist affirms their devotion to God and acknowledges that nothing on earth is desired except God.
- Imagery:
  - The psalmist's state of grief, bitterness, and ignorance is depicted as a senseless and ignorant state, akin to that of a brute beast.
  - God holding the psalmist by their right hand symbolizes divine guidance, support, and protection.
  - God's counsel is portrayed as a guiding force that leads the psalmist towards glory and eternal life.
  - The affirmation of devotion to God and the acknowledgment that nothing on earth is desired besides God highlights the psalmist's recognition of the ultimate worth and fulfillment found in a relationship with God.

## Scene 8: The Fate of the Wicked and the Believer's Hope

> "My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever. Those who are far from you will perish; you destroy all who are unfaithful to you. But as for me, it is good to be near God. I have made the Sovereign Lord my refuge; I will tell of all your deeds." - Psalm 73:26-28 (NIV)

- Scene:
  - The psalmist acknowledges the frailty of their own flesh and heart, but finds strength in God who is their eternal portion.
  - The fate of those who are far from God and unfaithful to Him is contrasted with the psalmist's nearness to God and security in Him.
  - The psalmist declares the goodness of being near God and affirms their trust in God as their refuge.
  - The psalmist expresses their commitment to proclaiming God's deeds.
- Imagery:
  - The psalmist's recognition of their own physical and emotional frailty highlights the limitations of human strength and points to the reliance on God's strength.
  - God being the psalmist's portion forever symbolizes divine provision, sustenance, and inheritance.
  - The contrast between the fate of those who are far from God and the psalmist's nearness to God illustrates the consequences of faithfulness and unfaithfulness.
  - Making God the refuge and proclaiming God's deeds signify the psalmist's confidence in God's protection and their commitment to testify to God's greatness and faithfulness.

## Summary

The scenes in this Psalm depict the psalmist's struggle with the prosperity of the wicked and their own doubts and envy. Through vivid imagery, the Psalm portrays the carefree and prosperous life of the wicked, their arrogance and disregard for God, and the internal turmoil and confusion experienced by the psalmist. The Psalm then transitions to a resolution as the psalmist finds understanding and clarity in God's presence, recognizing the fragility of the wicked's success and the eternal worth of devotion to God. Trust in God's guidance, strength, and refuge become central themes, leading the psalmist to declare the goodness of being near God and to proclaim His deeds. Ultimately, the Psalm presents a journey from doubt and envy to faith and hope, highlighting the transformative power of a deep relationship with God.
